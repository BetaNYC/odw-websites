Open Data L-train Innovation Challenge
Fee for entry
Saturday, March 10
9:00 am to 7:00 pm
Manhattan
335 Madison Avenue New York, NY

Join designers, programmers, engineers, data scientists, transportation experts, and committed citizens to tackle one of several challenges facing the city as part of the L-train shutdown scheduled for April 2019.

The Open Data L-Train Innovation Challenge will open up the problem to the public and facilitate collaborative teams to solve a number of challenges posed by the shutdown through a sustainability lens. The shutdown will repair the tunnels damaged by Hurricane Sandy connecting Manhattan to Brooklyn and will last 15-18 months.

Roughly 400,000 people ride the L-train with 225,000 of them riding between Brooklyn and Manhattan daily.

Participants will hear the social, economic, and environmental challenges currently facing the city and pertaining to the L-train shutdown.

Participants will then select a challenge, form teams, review data sets and capture new ideas on how to mitigate sustainability impacts from the L-train shutdown. Data sets will be provided by the MTA, Taxi and Limousine Commission, NYNJ Port Authority, Waze, and MoDA. Expert mentors will support teams as they develop their solutions.

Objectives

  • Capture new ideas on how to mitigate sustainability impacts from the L-train shutdown.
  • Use open data to foster sustainable mobility
  • Work collaboratively with multiple city agencies, citizens and private sector for innovation

A panel of judges will select the best ideas. We will then share next steps on moving the best ideas forward to bring together open data, a sustainability lens and citizen-led innovation for a new way of approaching some of the challenges facing the city in the face of the L-train shutdown.

Agenda

9:00 - 9:30 - Registration and breakfast

9:30 - 10:00 - Kickoff (agenda and objectives)

10:00 - 11:00 - Keynote, L-train shutdown background and challenges from a sustainability perspective (including a social, environmental and economic)

11:00 - 12:00 - Choose teams, challenges and initial idea generation

12:00 - 13:00 - Lunch

13:00 - 14:30 - Work (continue ideation and research)

14:30 - 15:00 - Break to share ideas in plenary

15:00 - 17:00 - Work (prototyping)

17:00 - 18:00 - Presentations and judging

18:00 - 19:00 - Happy hour and showcase

The all-day event is part of NYC Open Data Week and is made possible through partnerships with Grand Central Tech Hub, Dell, the Department of Transportation, and the Mayorâ€™s Office of Data Analytics (MODA) and many more contributors.

Open Data on School Bullying
Free
Tuesday, March 6
5:00 pm to 7:00 pm
Manhattan
101 6th Avenue, 23rd Floor New York, NY

School bullying and harassment are serious issues that affect the safety of young students. NYC public schools have taken steps to address these issues with an $8M plan that includes funding to launch an online portal for families to report instances of bullying, harassment, or discrimination. Though that data is not yet available, the Data Clinic, Two Sigma's pro-bono data science and technology arm, set out to examine the existing publicly-available datasets on bullying and harassment in NYC public schools. These sources include the NYC School Survey from the NYC Open Data Portal and federal data on bullying and harassment in NYC schools as reported to the Office for Civil Rights. The overall goal of this project was to explore and evaluate the differences and similarities between different sources of open data. For example, what are the questions being asked in the different surveys to capture similar issues surrounding bullying and harassment in NYC public schools? Are there associations or inconsistencies between the two surveys?

This 2-part event will begin with a presentation by the Data Clinic covering the methodology and challenges in accessing and merging datasets as well as initial findings and potential next steps, followed by a panel discussion highlighting different perspectives on education survey data. Topics for discussion may include:

Is it useful to combine local data with federal data on NYC schools? Why might there be discrepancies between the two data sources? How should the public best use and interpret data from different sources? What are some ways to make the data easier to use and understand? What are some other resources (data or otherwise) the public can access to investigate school bullying and harassment?

Maximizing the Value of Open Data APIs with Low Code Development Tools
Free
Thursday, March 8
5:00 pm to 5:45 pm
Manhattan
655 West 34th Street New York, NY

Many US cities offer comprehensive open data portals, with APIs that provide information on various types of government activities. Making municipal data publicly available makes local governments more accessible and accountable to the communities they serve. It also increases opportunities for the public to interact with public agencies. And given the right tools to mine and analyze this data, businesses and regular citizens just might develop new and innovative products and apps that take full advantage of the data provided.

In this session, we'll take look at a few of the public data sets provided by the City of New York, which can be accessed via:

https://opendata.cityofnewyork.us/

We will show how anyone can use Cloud-based Low Code tools, like Oracle Application Express (APEX) to easily build powerful reporting and data analysis tools that tap into data like municipal expenditures, 311 service requests, demographic and education data.

In cities made of brick and mortar, steel and concrete - digital technologies represent an added layer of information that brings meaning to the everyday, human dimension of cities.
